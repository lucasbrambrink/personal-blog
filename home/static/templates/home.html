Algorithms

Let's face it: algorithm challenges are fun. Beyond their utility as programmatic excercises, they sharpen our problem solving abilities. It is often said that problem solving is the main ingredient to successful software development.
"We should be solving problems, not building features. Features are attributes, not objectives."

As such, algorithm challenges force us to:

  1. Figure out exactly what components need to be addressed. Knowing this alone is invaluable.
  2. Think about how the ideal solution would behave.
  3. Iterate! Build with purpose, and fail quickly.

"As human beings, we get better at what we practice. No matter what it is. If you practice methodology X, you will get good at it. Therefore, I would like you to ask yourself, where do you think there is more leverage? I don't care what X is. Pick any X you want. Would you rather be good at it or the general skill of solving problems?

- Rich Hickey

Project Euler

There are many sources online for fun and quick algorithm challenges to get our code-juices flowing and keep our CPUs busy (ex. codeeval.org and coderbyte.com). These are fine -- indeed, they form the foundation of my programming career, and as such I have a deep admiration for them. I remember my first is_prime_number(num), and running through the apartment late at night when all my assert statements passed. From that point on I was hooked on programming!

However, I want to point out that Project Euler is slightly different from most other, perhaps more popular sources. Most PE problem cannot be solved by brute force. This constraint alone is so significant to our approach that one has no choice but to earnestly think about the problem.

From their website (projecteuler.net):

"Project Euler is a series of challenging mathematical/computer programming problems that will require more than just mathematical insights to solve. Although mathematics will help you arrive at elegant and efficient methods, the use of a computer and programming skills will be required to solve most problems."

“If I had an hour to solve a problem I'd spend 55 minutes thinking about the problem and 5 minutes thinking about solutions.”
-- Albert Einstein

Intruiged? Let's jump right in!

Large Sum (Problem 13)

"Work out the sum of the following one-hundred 50-digit numbers."

37107287533902102798797998220837590246510135740250
46376937677490009712648124896970078050417018260538
74324986199524741059474233309513058123726617309629
etc.

A fifty digit number, 10E50, is much larger than a conventional int (maximum value 2^63 - 1, which is ~10E20). Summing about 100 of these numbers puts our answer in the 10E52 range, and, while Python could perhaps handle such a number (as a long), let's do this analytically. What if we had to multiply these numbers?

How could we do this? Well, let's do it the same way that we learning summation in elementary school. We work backwards, digit by digit, and carry the one! Except in this case, our remainder is much, much larger than 1...

One could make the argument here that a purely functional approach might be better suited for this problem, but since I like object-oriented programming I will use it for this example.
